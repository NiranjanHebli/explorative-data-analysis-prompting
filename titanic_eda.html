Cabin: Critically Missing
77.1% of Cabin values are missing — this column cannot be used directly for modeling without extensive imputation strategies. The missing pattern likely correlates with lower-class passengers who had shared or unrecorded berths.

Actionable Insights & Findings

The most important patterns for understanding — and predicting — survival.

1
"Women & Children First" is Confirmed
The data strongly validates this protocol. Female survival was 74.2% vs 18.9% for males. Children ≤12 had 57.9% survival — the highest of any age group. Sex is the single most predictive feature in this dataset.
2
Class Determines Fate
1st class survival: 63% · 2nd class: 47% · 3rd class: 24%. Lower-class passengers had fewer routes to lifeboat decks, less time to react, and faced structural disadvantages that turned class into a life-or-death variable.
3
Fare as a Wealth Proxy
Survivors paid on average £48.40 — more than double the £22.12 non-survivors paid. Since Fare strongly correlates with Pclass, this reflects the class survival disparity rather than being an independent predictor.
4
Optimal Family Size: 2–4
Passengers in small families (2–4 members) had 55–72% survival rates. Solo travelers (30%) and large families (≤20%) both did poorly. The sweet spot suggests family support aided evacuation without creating coordination bottlenecks.
5
Embarkation Port Reflects Class
Cherbourg had 55.4% survival vs 33.7% for Southampton — not because of the port itself, but because Cherbourg attracted more wealthy 1st-class passengers. Port acts as a class proxy, not an independent survival factor.
6
Cabin Data is Compromised
With 77.1% of Cabin values missing, this field cannot be used reliably. However, having a recorded Cabin likely correlates with 1st-class status and survival — missingness itself is a meaningful signal worth encoding as a binary feature.
