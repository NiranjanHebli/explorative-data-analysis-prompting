Section 02
Missing Value Analysis

Three features carry missing data, with severity ranging from negligible to severe. Cabin is practically unusable at 77.1% missingness — only 204 of 891 records have a cabin designation, and this absence is strongly non-random: 81.5% of first-class passengers have recorded cabins versus just 2.4% of third-class passengers. Age is missing for 177 passengers (19.9%), making it a candidate for median or model-based imputation rather than removal. Embarked is missing for only 2 records (0.2%), trivially addressable via mode imputation.

Section 04
Correlation Findings

Pairwise correlations with the survival target reveal that passenger class and fare carry the most predictive signal among numeric features. Pclass shows a negative correlation of −0.338 (higher class number = lower cabin class = worse outcomes), while Fare shows a positive correlation of +0.257 — both reflecting socioeconomic privilege. Age has a weak negative correlation (−0.077), and family-size features (SibSp, Parch) show minimal direct linear relationship with survival, obscuring the non-linear sweet spot at mid-size families.

Section 05
5 Critical Insights

The following findings represent the most statistically and practically significant patterns within the dataset.

01
Gender Was the Dominant Survival Factor
Female passengers survived at a rate of 74.2% compared to just 18.9% for males — a 3.9× multiplier that dwarfs every other variable in the dataset. Of 314 women, 233 survived; of 577 men, only 109 did. This reflects the "women and children first" maritime protocol enforced during evacuation. Sex is the single most predictive feature for survival and should be the first variable included in any model.

02
Class Determined Life Expectancy Aboard
First-class passengers survived at 63.0%, second class at 47.3%, and third class at just 24.2% — a clear, monotonic socioeconomic gradient. Third-class passengers were physically located deep in the ship and faced structural barriers to reaching lifeboats. The 38.8 percentage-point gap between first and third class represents one of the starkest illustrations of class-based inequality in recorded maritime history.

03
Children Under 10 Had the Highest Survival Rate
Passengers under age 10 survived at 59.4% — the highest rate of any age group — consistent with evacuation priority given to young children. Survival rates declined steadily through adolescence (42.7% for ages 10–18) and middle age, reaching 34.4% for passengers over 50. Notably, the very young benefited despite being overrepresented in third class, suggesting the "children first" protocol had real cross-class effect for the youngest passengers.
04
Traveling Alone Was Deadly; Large Families Were Too
Solo travelers had a survival rate of just 30.4%. Passengers with small families of 2–4 members fared significantly better: family-size-2 passengers survived at 55.3%, family-size-4 at 72.4%. However, this inverted sharply for large families: groups of 5–6 survived at only 13.6%, and groups of 8 or 11 had a 0% survival rate. This non-linear pattern suggests large families had difficulty coordinating evacuation while small groups could assist each other.
05
Port of Embarkation Reveals Hidden Class Composition
Passengers who embarked at Cherbourg (France) survived at 55.4%, compared to 38.9% from Queenstown (Ireland) and 33.7% from Southampton (England). This appears to be a confounding effect rather than a direct cause: Cherbourg passengers were disproportionately first-class and wealthier. When controlling for class, the embarkation effect diminishes substantially — making it a useful proxy feature in models but not a causal predictor of survival.

Section 06
Modeling Recommendations

Based on this analysis, the highest-value features for predictive modeling are Sex (strongest single predictor), Pclass (strong class-based gradient), Fare (correlated with class and survival), and Age (moderate signal after imputation). Family size features benefit from feature engineering into a combined FamilySize variable, or a binary "IsAlone" flag. Cabin should be converted to a binary "HasCabin" indicator rather than used as a raw categorical. Age should be imputed via median stratified by Sex and Pclass. Title extraction from passenger names (Mr., Mrs., Miss, Master) provides a high-signal engineered feature that captures both age and gender in compressed form — Masters (young boys) survived at 57.5% while Reverends had a 0% survival rate.

"The Titanic dataset is a microcosm of Edwardian social hierarchy made viscerally legible through survival data: wealth and gender were quite literally life-or-death."
